NICE Guideline NG128 · Published 1 May 2019 · Last updated 13 April 2022

Stroke and TIA in Over 16s

Clinical revision summary for healthcare professionals and students

Specialty: Stroke Medicine · Neurology · Emergency Medicine Age group: > 16 years Source: www.nice.org.uk/guidance/ng128
Three Presentations at a Glance

TIA

Transient ischaemic attack. Aspirin 300 mg immediately, specialist assessment within 24 h. Do NOT use CT routinely or ABCD2 scoring. Consider MRI same-day in the TIA clinic. Carotid endarterectomy if symptomatic stenosis 50–99% NASCET (or 70–99% ECST).

Acute ischaemic stroke

Thrombolysis (alteplase or tenecteplase) within 4.5 h; thrombectomy within 6 h proximal anterior, or 6–24 h with limited infarct core. Aspirin 300 mg for 2 weeks once ICH excluded.

Haemorrhagic stroke (ICH)

Rapid BP lowering (target ≤140 mmHg systolic) if within 6 h and SBP 150–220 mmHg, in absence of exclusion criteria. Reverse warfarin with PCC + IV vitamin K. Surgery only in selected indications.

1

Overview & Scope

This guideline covers interventions in the acute stage of a stroke or TIA in people aged > 16. It offers the best clinical advice on diagnosis and acute management within the first 48 hours after onset of symptoms.

NG128 replaces CG68 and is the basis of QS2. Three parallel clinical presentations — TIA, acute ischaemic stroke, and haemorrhagic stroke (intracerebral haemorrhage) — are managed in this single guideline. Long-term rehabilitation is covered separately.

2

Rapid Recognition & Initial TIA Management

Recognition tools

1
Outside hospital: USE a validated tool such as FAST (Face Arm Speech Test) to screen people with sudden onset of neurological symptoms.
2
Exclude hypoglycaemia as a cause of sudden onset of neurological symptoms.
3
In the emergency department: USE a validated tool such as ROSIER (Recognition of Stroke in the Emergency Room) to establish the diagnosis rapidly.

Initial management of suspected/confirmed TIA

IMMEDIATE

OFFER aspirin 300 mg daily (unless contraindicated) to people with suspected TIA, started immediately. REFER immediately for specialist assessment and investigation within 24 h of symptom onset. OFFER secondary prevention in addition to aspirin as soon as the TIA diagnosis is confirmed.

DO NOT

Use scoring systems such as ABCD2 to assess risk of subsequent stroke or to inform urgency of referral for people with suspected or confirmed TIA.

3

Imaging — Suspected TIA / Non-Disabling Stroke

DO NOT

Offer CT brain scanning to people with a suspected TIA unless there is clinical suspicion of an alternative diagnosis that CT could detect.

  • After specialist assessment in the TIA clinic, CONSIDER MRI (including diffusion-weighted and blood-sensitive sequences) to determine the territory of ischaemia or to detect haemorrhage or alternative pathologies. If MRI is done, perform it on the same day as the assessment.

Carotid imaging & endarterectomy decision

Everyone with TIA who, after specialist assessment, is a candidate for carotid endarterectomy should have urgent carotid imaging. Carotid imaging reports must clearly state which criteria (NASCET or ECST) were used.

Specialist Care & Brain Imaging in Acute Stroke

DIRECT ADMISSION

Admit everyone with suspected stroke DIRECTLY to a specialist acute stroke unit after initial assessment — from the community, emergency department or outpatient clinics. An acute stroke unit = a discrete area staffed by a specialist stroke MDT with monitoring/rehabilitation equipment, with regular MDT goal-setting meetings.

Immediate non-enhanced CT — indications

PERFORM brain imaging immediately (within 1 hour) with a non-enhanced CT if any of:

  • Indications for thrombolysis or thrombectomy
  • On anticoagulant treatment
  • A known bleeding tendency
  • Depressed level of consciousness (GCS < 13)
  • Unexplained progressive or fluctuating symptoms
  • Papilloedema, neck stiffness or fever
  • Severe headache at onset of stroke symptoms
  • If thrombectomy might be indicated: perform CT contrast angiography following the initial non-enhanced CT.
  • Add CT perfusion imaging (or MR equivalent) if thrombectomy might be indicated beyond 6 hours of symptom onset.
  • Without immediate-imaging indications: scan as soon as possible and within 24 hours of symptom onset.
5

Thrombolysis — Acute Ischaemic Stroke

OFFER

Thrombolysis with alteplase (TA264, 2012) or tenecteplase (TA990, 2024) for acute ischaemic stroke within 4.5 hours of symptom onset, when intracranial haemorrhage has been excluded.

6

Thrombectomy — Acute Ischaemic Stroke

Thrombectomy decision pathway

1
≤ 6 h symptom onset + acute ischaemic stroke + confirmed proximal anterior circulation occlusion on CTA/MRA → OFFER THROMBECTOMY as soon as possible, with IV thrombolysis if not contraindicated and within licensed window.
2
Last known to be well between 6 and 24 h (including wake-up strokes) + proximal anterior circulation occlusion + potential to salvage brain tissue (CT perfusion or DWI showing limited infarct core) → OFFER THROMBECTOMY.
3
Up to 24 h from last known well + proximal posterior circulation occlusion (basilar or posterior cerebral artery) + potential to salvage brain tissue → CONSIDER THROMBECTOMY + IV thrombolysis where appropriate.

Patient-selection criteria for thrombectomy

  • Pre-stroke functional status: modified Rankin scale (mRS) < 3
  • NIHSS score > 5
Off-label device note (May 2019)

Not all devices with a CE mark for thrombectomy were intended by the manufacturer for use as recommended here. Follow professional guidance, take full responsibility, obtain informed consent. MHRA: use CE-marked devices for their intended purpose where possible.

Aspirin, Anticoagulation & Statins

Aspirin for acute ischaemic stroke

OFFER (within 24 h, ICH excluded)

Offer aspirin 300 mg as soon as possible (within 24 h) once intracerebral haemorrhage is excluded by brain imaging:

  • Aspirin 300 mg orally if no dysphagia
  • Aspirin 300 mg rectally or by enteral tube if dysphagia
  • Continue daily 300 mg for 2 weeks, then start definitive long-term antithrombotic treatment (or earlier if discharging before 2 weeks)
  • Add a PPI if previous dyspepsia associated with aspirin
  • Offer an alternative antiplatelet if allergic or genuinely intolerant of aspirin
Aspirin intolerance — definition

Either proven hypersensitivity to aspirin-containing medicines, OR a history of severe dyspepsia induced by low-dose aspirin.

Anticoagulation — by clinical context

DO NOT (routine)

Routinely use anticoagulation treatment for acute stroke. (There may be a subgroup at higher VTE risk than haemorrhagic-transformation risk — complete leg paralysis, previous VTE, dehydration, malignancy, current/recent smoker — kept under regular review if given prophylactic anticoagulation.)

ScenarioTreatment
Cerebral venous sinus thrombosis (incl. with secondary cerebral haemorrhage) Full-dose anticoagulation — initial full-dose heparin then warfarin (INR 2–3)
Stroke from arterial dissection Anticoagulants OR antiplatelet agents
Antiphospholipid syndrome + acute ischaemic stroke Same as acute ischaemic stroke without APS
Disabling ischaemic stroke + AF Aspirin 300 mg for first 2 weeks before anticoagulation considered
Prosthetic valves + disabling cerebral infarction at risk of haemorrhagic transformation Stop anticoagulation for 1 week + substitute aspirin 300 mg
Ischaemic stroke + symptomatic proximal DVT/PE Anticoagulation (preferred over aspirin) unless contraindications
Haemorrhagic stroke + symptomatic DVT/PE Anticoagulation OR caval filter

Reversal of anticoagulation in haemorrhagic stroke

WARFARIN REVERSAL

Return clotting levels to normal as soon as possible in primary intracerebral haemorrhage on warfarin (elevated INR) — use a combination of prothrombin complex concentrate (PCC) + IV vitamin K.

For DOAC reversal, see the MHRA safety advice on DOACs for a list of reversal agents.

Statins

DO NOT (immediate)

Immediate initiation of statin treatment is not recommended in acute stroke. (Committee consensus: safe to start statins after 48 hours.) Continue statin treatment in people already receiving statins.

Maintenance / Restoration of Homeostasis

Supplemental oxygen

  • GIVE supplemental oxygen only if oxygen saturation drops below 95%. Routine oxygen is NOT recommended in acute stroke patients who are not hypoxic.
Pulse oximeter accuracy

Some pulse oximeters can underestimate or overestimate oxygen saturation, especially borderline values. Overestimation has been reported in people with dark skin. See the NHS England Patient Safety Alert on the risk of harm from inappropriate placement of pulse oximeter probes.

Blood sugar control

  • Maintain blood glucose 4–11 mmol/L in people with acute stroke.
  • Provide optimal insulin therapy (achievable with IV insulin and glucose) to all adults with type 1 diabetes with threatened or actual stroke. Critical care and ED should have a protocol.

BP control — acute intracerebral haemorrhage

1
Within 6 h of symptom onset + systolic BP 150–220 mmHg + no exclusions → CONSIDER RAPID BP LOWERING.
2
Beyond 6 h OR systolic > 220 mmHg + no exclusions → CONSIDER on a case-by-case basis taking into account risk of harm.
3
Target: systolic BP ≤ 140 mmHg, ensuring the magnitude drop does not exceed 60 mmHg in 1 hour of starting treatment.
EXCLUSIONS — DO NOT lower BP rapidly

Do not rapidly lower BP in people who:

  • Have an underlying structural cause (e.g. tumour, AVM, aneurysm)
  • Have a Glasgow Coma Scale score below 6
  • Are going to have early neurosurgery to evacuate the haematoma
  • Have a massive haematoma with a poor expected prognosis

For young people aged 16 or 17 with acute intracerebral haemorrhage (no exclusions), seek advice from a paediatric specialist when considering BP lowering.

BP control — acute ischaemic stroke

Anti-hypertensive treatment is recommended ONLY in a hypertensive emergency with one or more of:

  • Hypertensive encephalopathy
  • Hypertensive nephropathy
  • Hypertensive cardiac failure / myocardial infarction
  • Aortic dissection
  • Pre-eclampsia / eclampsia

BP reduction to ≤ 185/110 mmHg should be considered in people who are candidates for IV thrombolysis.

Nutrition & Hydration

Swallowing assessment pathway

1
On admission: screen swallowing by an appropriately trained healthcare professional before any oral food, fluid or medication.
→ Problems identified:
2
Specialist assessment of swallowing — preferably within 24 h of admission, and not more than 72 h afterwards.
→ Suspected aspiration OR tube feeding/modification ≥ 3 days:
3
Re-assess + consider instrumental examination, and refer for dietary advice.
→ Unable to take adequate oral intake:
4
NG tube within 24 h of admission unless they have had thrombolysis. Consider nasal bridle tube or gastrostomy if unable to tolerate NG. Refer for detailed nutritional assessment; review oral medication formulation/route.

Malnutrition screening

  • Screen all hospital inpatients on admission for malnutrition risk; repeat weekly for inpatients.
  • Screening should assess BMI and % unintentional weight loss; consider time over which intake has been reduced and likelihood of future impaired intake. MUST (Malnutrition Universal Screening Tool) may be used.
  • Be aware that dysphagia, poor oral health and reduced ability to self-feed will affect nutrition.
DO NOT (routine)

Routine nutritional supplementation is not recommended for people with acute stroke who are adequately nourished on admission.

START nutrition support for people at risk of malnutrition — oral nutritional supplements, specialist dietary advice and/or tube feeding.

Hydration

Assess on admission; review hydration regularly; manage so that normal hydration is maintained.

10

Positioning & Early Mobilisation

  • Assess individual clinical needs and personal preferences to determine optimal head position — comfort, physical/cognitive abilities, postural control.
  • HELP people sit out of bed, stand or walk as soon as their clinical condition permits, as part of an active management programme in a specialist stroke unit.
DO NOT (first 24 h)

If people need help to sit out of bed, stand or walk, do NOT offer high-intensity mobilisation in the first 24 h after symptom onset. ('High-intensity mobilisation' = the AVERT-trial intervention: ≥ 3 additional out-of-bed sessions vs usual care, focused on sitting/standing/walking, beginning within 24 h.)

11

Avoiding Aspiration Pneumonia

To avoid aspiration pneumonia, give food, fluids and medication to people with dysphagia in a form that can be swallowed without aspiration, after specialist assessment of swallowing.

12

Surgery — Haemorrhage & Decompressive Hemicraniectomy

Acute intracerebral haemorrhage

  • Stroke services should agree protocols for monitoring, referring and transferring people to regional neurosurgical centres for the management of symptomatic hydrocephalus.
  • Monitor people with intracerebral haemorrhage by specialists in neurosurgical or stroke care for deterioration in function; refer immediately for brain imaging when necessary.
  • Previously fit people should be considered for surgical intervention following primary intracerebral haemorrhage if they have hydrocephalus.
RARELY REQUIRE SURGERY

People with any of the following rarely require surgical intervention and should receive medical treatment initially:

  • Small deep haemorrhages
  • Lobar haemorrhage without either hydrocephalus or rapid neurological deterioration
  • A large haemorrhage and significant comorbidities before the stroke
  • GCS below 8, unless this is because of hydrocephalus
  • Posterior fossa haemorrhage

Decompressive hemicraniectomy

CONSIDER (within 48 h)

CONSIDER decompressive hemicraniectomy (performed within 48 h of symptom onset) for people who meet ALL the following:

  • Clinical deficits suggesting infarction in the MCA territory, with NIHSS > 15
  • Decreased level of consciousness: NIHSS item 1a score ≥ 1
  • CT signs of an infarct involving ≥ 50% of the MCA territory — with or without additional infarction in the anterior or posterior cerebral artery on the same side — OR infarct volume > 145 cm³ on diffusion-weighted MRI

Discuss risks and benefits with people or their family members/carers, taking into account their functional status before the stroke and their wishes/preferences. NICE has produced patient decision aids on decompressive hemicraniectomy. People referred for decompressive hemicraniectomy should be monitored by appropriately trained professionals skilled in neurological assessment.

Terms Used in This Guideline

High-intensity mobilisation

Refers to the very early mobilisation intervention from the AVERT trial. Includes mobilisation that begins within the first 24 hours of stroke onset, includes at least 3 additional out-of-bed sessions compared with usual care, and focuses on sitting, standing and walking (i.e. out-of-bed) activity.

Aspirin intolerance

Either proven hypersensitivity to aspirin-containing medicines, OR a history of severe dyspepsia induced by low-dose aspirin.

"Immediately" (for imaging — 1.3.2)

'Ideally the next slot and definitely within 1 hour, whichever is sooner'.
