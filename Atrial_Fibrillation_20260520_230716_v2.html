NICE Guideline NG196 · Published 27 April 2021 · Last updated 30 June 2021

Atrial Fibrillation: Diagnosis and Management

Clinical revision summary for healthcare professionals and students

Specialty: Cardiology · Arrhythmia Age group: ≥18 years Source: www.nice.org.uk/guidance/ng196
1

Overview & Context

Atrial fibrillation (AF) is the most common heart rhythm disorder, affecting approximately 2% of the adult population, and its prevalence is rising. It is responsible for approximately 20% of all strokes and is associated with increased mortality. Men are more commonly affected than women; prevalence rises with age, and with underlying heart disease, diabetes, obesity and hypertension.

This guideline (NG196, 2021) covers diagnosing and managing AF in adults aged ≥18 years, including paroxysmal, persistent and permanent AF and atrial flutter. It does not apply to people with congenital heart disease precipitating AF. NG196 replaces CG180 and is the basis of QS93. It should be read alongside TA275 (apixaban), TA249 (dabigatran), TA355 (edoxaban), TA256 (rivaroxaban) and TA197 (dronedarone).

MHRA advice — warfarin during COVID-19

See MHRA advice on warfarin and other anticoagulants — monitoring of patients during the COVID-19 pandemic, including reports of supratherapeutic anticoagulation with warfarin.

The treatment aim is to prevent complications (particularly stroke) and alleviate symptoms using anticoagulants, antiarrhythmics, electrical cardioversion and catheter or surgical ablation.

Detection & Diagnosis

When to suspect AF

OFFER manual pulse palpation to assess for an irregular pulse in anyone presenting with any of the following:

  • breathlessness
  • palpitations
  • syncope or dizziness
  • chest discomfort
  • stroke or transient ischaemic attack

Confirming the diagnosis

1
OFFER manual pulse palpation if AF is suspected.
→ If an irregular pulse is detected:
2
PERFORM a 12-lead ECG to make the diagnosis (with or without symptoms).
→ If 12-lead ECG is normal but paroxysmal AF is suspected:
3a
USE a 24-hour ambulatory ECG monitor if asymptomatic episodes are suspected, or if symptomatic episodes are <24 h apart.
3b
USE an ambulatory ECG monitor, event recorder or other ECG technology for a longer appropriate period if symptomatic episodes are >24 h apart.
Why 12-lead ECG?

The committee specifically chose 12-lead ECG to confirm AF in order to prevent reliance on less accurate single-lead or mobile devices. NICE has separate diagnostics guidance on lead-I ECG devices for single time-point testing in primary care.

3

Assessment of Stroke & Bleeding Risk

Stroke risk — CHA2DS2-VASc

USE the CHA2DS2-VASc score to assess stroke risk in anyone with:

  • symptomatic or asymptomatic paroxysmal, persistent or permanent AF
  • atrial flutter
  • a continuing risk of arrhythmia recurrence after cardioversion or catheter ablation

Bleeding risk — ORBIT

ASSESS bleeding risk both before starting anticoagulation and when reviewing people already taking it. USE the ORBIT bleeding risk score — evidence shows it has higher accuracy at predicting absolute bleeding risk than other tools. Other tools may be used during a transition period until ORBIT is embedded in clinical pathways.

Accurate knowledge of bleeding risk supports shared decision making: it can increase patient confidence and willingness to accept treatment when risk is low, and prompt discussion of risk modification when risk is high.

Modifiable risk factors for bleeding

OFFER monitoring and support to address the following modifiable risk factors:

  • Uncontrolled hypertension (see NICE NG136 on hypertension)
  • Poor INR control in patients on vitamin K antagonists
  • Concurrent medication — antiplatelets, SSRIs, NSAIDs
  • Harmful alcohol consumption
  • Reversible causes of anaemia

Discussing the results

Discuss the results of stroke and bleeding risk assessments with the person, taking their specific comorbidities and individual preferences into account. Follow NICE on enabling patients to actively participate in their care.

4

Assessment of Cardiac Function

When to perform TTE

PERFORM transthoracic echocardiography (TTE) in people with AF when:

  • a baseline echocardiogram is important for long-term management
  • a rhythm-control strategy that includes cardioversion (electrical or pharmacological) is being considered
  • there is a high risk or suspicion of underlying structural or functional heart disease (e.g. heart failure, heart murmur) that influences management — for example, choice of antiarrhythmic drug
  • refinement of clinical risk stratification for antithrombotic therapy is needed
DO NOT

Routinely perform TTE solely for further stroke risk stratification when the decision to start anticoagulation has already been made on appropriate clinical criteria.

When to perform TOE

PERFORM transoesophageal echocardiography (TOE) when:

  • TTE demonstrates an abnormality (e.g. valvular heart disease) warranting further specific assessment
  • TTE is technically difficult/of questionable quality and cardiac abnormalities need to be excluded
  • TOE-guided cardioversion is being considered
5

Personalised Package of Care

OFFER a personalised package of care to every person with AF. Ensure it is documented and delivered, and that it covers:

  • Stroke awareness and measures to prevent stroke
  • Rate control
  • Assessment of symptoms for rhythm control
  • Who to contact for advice if needed
  • Psychological support if needed
  • Up-to-date and comprehensive education and information on:
    • cause, effects and possible complications of AF
    • management of rate and rhythm control
    • anticoagulation
    • practical advice on anticoagulation (see NICE on venous thromboembolic diseases)
    • support networks (e.g. cardiovascular charities)
Cross-references

Follow NICE's guidelines on shared decision making, medicines adherence, and medicines optimisation to support adherence and ensure safe, effective use.

6

Referral for Specialised Management

REFER

Refer promptly at any stage if treatment fails to control AF symptoms and more specialised management is needed. Referral should be within 4 weeks after the failed treatment or after recurrence of AF following cardioversion.

7

Stroke Prevention — Anticoagulation

Discussing anticoagulation

When discussing benefits and risks, use clinical risk profiles and personal preferences:

  • For most people, the benefit of anticoagulation outweighs the bleeding risk.
  • For people at increased bleeding risk, the benefit may not always outweigh the risk — careful monitoring of bleeding risk is important.

Take any contraindications for each drug into account and follow guidance in the BNF and MHRA advice on direct-acting oral anticoagulants (in particular dosages in renal impairment, reversal agents and monitoring).

Choosing the anticoagulant

OFFER

Offer a direct-acting oral anticoagulant (DOAC) — apixaban, dabigatran, edoxaban or rivaroxaban — to anyone with AF and a CHA2DS2-VASc score ≥2, taking the risk of bleeding into account.

CONSIDER a DOAC for men with AF and CHA2DS2-VASc score = 1, taking bleeding risk into account.

OFFER a vitamin K antagonist (VKA) if DOACs are contraindicated, not tolerated or not suitable.

For people already taking a VKA and stable, continue current medication and discuss the option of switching at their next routine appointment, taking into account their time in therapeutic range.

DO NOT

Offer stroke prevention therapy with anticoagulation to people aged <65 years with AF and no risk factors other than their sex (i.e. very low risk — CHA2DS2-VASc 0 for men or 1 for women).

DO NOT

Withhold anticoagulation solely because of a person's age or their risk of falls.

DOAC treatment options (TAs brought into the guideline)

Each DOAC is recommended within its marketing authorisation in non-valvular AF, after informed discussion of risks and benefits compared with warfarin and the other DOACs. For people taking warfarin, consider switching taking into account their INR control.

Assessing VKA control — time in therapeutic range

Calculate the person's time in therapeutic range (TTR) at each visit. When calculating TTR:

  • Use a validated method (e.g. Rosendaal method for computer-assisted dosing, or proportion of tests in range for manual dosing).
  • Exclude measurements taken during the first 6 weeks of treatment.
  • Calculate TTR over a maintenance period of at least 6 months.

When to reassess anticoagulation

REASSESS anticoagulation if VKA control is poor, defined by any of the following within the past 6 months:

  • 2 INR values >5, OR 1 INR value >8
  • 2 INR values <1.5
  • TTR <65%

When reassessing, address contributing factors: cognitive function, adherence, illness, interacting drug therapy, and lifestyle (diet, alcohol). If poor control cannot be improved, evaluate alternative stroke-prevention strategies with the person.

Antiplatelets

DO NOT

Offer aspirin monotherapy solely for stroke prevention in people with AF.

For people post-MI also requiring anticoagulation, see NICE NG185 on acute coronary syndromes (antiplatelet therapy for people with an ongoing separate indication for anticoagulation).

Review intervals

When age 65 reached, or new risk factor develops
Review stroke risk in anyone not on anticoagulant — new risk factors include diabetes, heart failure, peripheral arterial disease, coronary heart disease, stroke/TIA/systemic thromboembolism.
Annually
For people not on anticoagulant because of bleeding risk: review stroke and bleeding risks every year and document.
At least annually (more often if needed)
For people on anticoagulant: review the need for, and quality of, anticoagulation. Take MHRA advice on DOACs into account (bleeding risk, renal function monitoring).

Left atrial appendage occlusion (LAAO)

CONSIDER LAAO if anticoagulation is contraindicated or not tolerated, and discuss the benefits and risks with the person. See NICE IPG on percutaneous occlusion of the left atrial appendage in non-valvular AF.

DO NOT

Offer LAAO as an alternative to anticoagulation unless anticoagulation is contraindicated or not tolerated.

VKA self-monitoring

NICE has produced diagnostics guidance on self-monitoring coagulation status using point-of-care coagulometers (CoaguChek XS system) in AF and heart valve disease.

Rate Control (Non-acute)

First-line strategy

OFFER

Rate control as the first-line treatment strategy for AF.

Exceptions — rate control is not first-line if any of the following apply:

  • AF has a reversible cause
  • Heart failure thought to be primarily caused by AF
  • New-onset AF
  • Atrial flutter whose condition is suitable for an ablation strategy to restore sinus rhythm
  • Rhythm control would be more suitable on clinical judgement

Choice of rate-control monotherapy

Patient scenarioMonotherapy choice
Default — choose based on symptoms, heart rate, comorbidities and preferences OFFER either a standard beta-blocker (not sotalol) OR a rate-limiting calcium-channel blocker — diltiazem or verapamil.
AF + concomitant heart failure Follow NICE NG106 chronic heart failure — use beta-blockers; avoid calcium-channel blockers.
Non-paroxysmal AF AND sedentary, OR other rate-limiting drugs ruled out CONSIDER digoxin monotherapy.

If monotherapy fails to control symptoms (and symptoms are due to poor ventricular rate control): CONSIDER combination therapy with any 2 of: a beta-blocker, diltiazem, digoxin.

Off-label use (April 2021)

The use of diltiazem for rate control in AF (alone or in combination) was off-label in April 2021. See NICE's information on prescribing medicines.

DO NOT

Offer amiodarone for long-term rate control. Amiodarone has serious side effects (thyroid, lung and nerve damage, many irreversible) and should only be used as an interim therapy (e.g. while waiting for cardioversion) — not usually for longer than 12 months.

9

Rhythm Control (Non-acute)

When to consider rhythm control

CONSIDER pharmacological and/or electrical rhythm control for people whose symptoms continue after heart rate is controlled, or for whom a rate-control strategy has not been successful. Assess the need for long-term rhythm-control drug treatment taking into account preferences, comorbidities, treatment risks and likelihood of recurrence.

Antiarrhythmic drug therapy

DO NOT

Offer class 1c antiarrhythmics (flecainide, propafenone) to people with known ischaemic or structural heart disease.

CONSIDER a standard beta-blocker (not sotalol) as first-line for long-term rhythm control unless contraindicated. If beta-blockers are contraindicated or unsuccessful, assess suitability of alternative drugs based on comorbidities.

CONSIDER amiodarone for people with left ventricular impairment or heart failure.

Dronedarone (TA197) — second-line option

Follow advice on dronedarone as a second-line option for long-term rhythm control after successful cardioversion.

TA197 — Dronedarone eligibility (ALL of the following)

Paroxysmal or persistent AF not controlled by first-line therapy (usually including beta-blockers) — i.e. as second-line after alternatives considered — AND

At least 1 of the following cardiovascular risk factors:

  • Hypertension requiring drugs of ≥2 different classes
  • Diabetes mellitus
  • Previous TIA, stroke or systemic embolism
  • Left atrial diameter ≥50 mm
  • Age ≥70 y

AND no left ventricular systolic dysfunction; AND no history of, or current, heart failure.

"No drug" and "pill-in-the-pocket" strategies

In people with infrequent paroxysms and few symptoms, or symptoms induced by known precipitants (e.g. alcohol, caffeine), CONSIDER a 'no drug treatment' strategy or a 'pill-in-the-pocket' strategy (antiarrhythmic taken only when an episode starts).

"Pill-in-the-pocket" eligibility — ALL must apply
  • No history of left ventricular dysfunction, valvular or ischaemic heart disease
  • History of infrequent symptomatic episodes of paroxysmal AF
  • Systolic BP >100 mmHg AND resting heart rate >70 bpm
  • Able to understand how and when to take the medication

Cardioversion

OFFER

For AF that has persisted >48 hours, offer electrical (not pharmacological) cardioversion.

CONSIDER amiodarone therapy starting 4 weeks before and continuing up to 12 months after electrical cardioversion to maintain sinus rhythm; discuss benefits and risks with the person.

For elective cardioversion of AF >48 h:

  • TOE-guided and conventional cardioversion are considered equally effective.
  • TOE-guided strategy should be considered when experienced staff and appropriate facilities are available, or if a minimal period of precardioversion anticoagulation is indicated due to patient choice or bleeding risk.

Left atrial ablation

1
Drug treatment unsuccessful, unsuitable or not tolerated in symptomatic paroxysmal or persistent AF.
→ First-line ablation choice:
2
CONSIDER radiofrequency point-by-point ablation.
→ If RF point-by-point is assessed as unsuitable:
3
CONSIDER cryoballoon ablation or laser balloon ablation.

When considering left atrial ablation, discuss risks and benefits and the person's preferences. In particular, explain that the procedure is not always effective and that symptom resolution may not be long-lasting.

CONSIDER left atrial surgical ablation at the same time as other cardiothoracic surgery for people with symptomatic AF.

Preventing recurrence after ablation

CONSIDER antiarrhythmic drug treatment for 3 months after left atrial ablation to prevent recurrence, taking preferences, risks and benefits into account. Reassess the need at 3 months.

Pace and ablate strategy

CONSIDER pacing and atrioventricular (AV) node ablation for people with permanent AF with symptoms or LV dysfunction thought to be caused by high ventricular rates. Reassess symptoms and the consequent need for AV-node ablation after pacing has been carried out and drug treatment further optimised.

CONSIDER left atrial catheter ablation BEFORE pacing and AV-node ablation for people with paroxysmal AF or heart failure caused by non-permanent (paroxysmal or persistent) AF.

Acute Presentation

DO NOT DELAY

In people with life-threatening haemodynamic instability caused by new-onset AF, carry out emergency electrical cardioversion without delaying to achieve anticoagulation.

Rate vs rhythm control — the 48-hour rule

For acute AF presentations without life-threatening instability, choice between rate and rhythm control depends on onset duration:

Onset durationAction
Onset < 48 h OFFER either rate or rhythm control.
Onset > 48 h or uncertain OFFER rate control.

Acute AF with decompensated heart failure

CAUTION

In people with AF presenting acutely with suspected concomitant acute decompensated heart failure: seek senior specialist input on the use of beta-blockers, and do not use calcium-channel blockers — they can cause further deterioration in pulmonary oedema caused by heart failure.

Pharmacological cardioversion choices

CONSIDER pharmacological or electrical cardioversion in new-onset AF when rhythm control is chosen. If pharmacological cardioversion is agreed:

  • No structural or ischaemic heart disease → OFFER a choice of flecainide or amiodarone.
  • Structural heart disease → OFFER amiodarone.
DO NOT

Offer magnesium or a calcium-channel blocker for pharmacological cardioversion.

When to delay cardioversion

If AF duration is >48 h or uncertain and long-term rhythm control is being considered, delay cardioversion until therapeutic anticoagulation has been maintained for a minimum of 3 weeks. Offer rate control during this period.

Anticoagulation in acute AF

In new-onset AF receiving no or subtherapeutic anticoagulation:

  • In the absence of contraindications, OFFER heparin at initial presentation.
  • Continue heparin until a full assessment has been made and appropriate antithrombotic therapy has been started, based on risk stratification.

In confirmed AF of recent onset (<48 h), OFFER oral anticoagulation if any of:

  • stable sinus rhythm is not successfully restored within the same 48-hour period
  • factors indicating a high risk of AF recurrence are present — history of failed cardioversion, structural heart disease, prolonged AF >12 months, previous recurrences
  • it is recommended by the stroke/bleeding risk assessment

If the precise time since onset is uncertain, offer oral anticoagulation as for persistent AF.

Initial Management of Stroke and AF

For initial management of stroke in a patient with AF, follow recommendation 1.4.17 in NICE NG128 on stroke and transient ischaemic attack in over 16s.

12

Postoperative AF — Prevention & Management

Preventing postoperative AF (cardiothoracic surgery)

In people having cardiothoracic surgery, reduce the risk of postoperative AF by offering one of:

  • Amiodarone
  • A standard beta-blocker (not sotalol)
  • A rate-limiting calcium-channel blocker — diltiazem or verapamil
DO NOT

Offer digoxin for postoperative AF prevention.

People already on beta-blocker therapy: continue this treatment unless contraindications develop (e.g. postoperative bradycardia or hypotension).

DO NOT

Start statins in people having cardiothoracic surgery solely to prevent postoperative AF. (People already on statins for other reasons should continue — see NICE on cardiovascular disease risk assessment and reduction.)

Off-label use (April 2021)

The use of diltiazem for postoperative AF prevention was off-label in April 2021.

Managing new-onset postoperative AF after cardiothoracic surgery

CONSIDER either a rhythm-control or rate-control strategy for initial treatment. If rhythm control is chosen, reassess the need for antiarrhythmic drug treatment at a suitable time point — usually around 6 weeks.

After non-cardiothoracic surgery

Manage postoperative AF after non-cardiothoracic surgery in the same way as new-onset AF with any other cause.

Antithrombotic therapy

In the prophylaxis and management of postoperative AF, use appropriate antithrombotic therapy and correct identifiable causes (e.g. electrolyte imbalance, hypoxia).

13

Stopping Anticoagulation

DO NOT

Stop anticoagulation solely because AF is no longer detectable. Paroxysmal AF is not always detectable, and the underlying stroke risk may persist.

Base decisions to stop anticoagulation on:

  • A reassessment of stroke and bleeding risk using CHA2DS2-VASc and ORBIT
  • A discussion of the person's preferences
14

Terms Used in This Guideline

People with atrial fibrillation presenting acutely

People presenting with AF of definite recent onset, or with destabilisation of existing AF. Does NOT include AF discovered incidentally (e.g. through pulse palpation before a routine blood pressure measurement).

Pill-in-the-pocket strategy

The person self-manages paroxysmal AF by taking antiarrhythmic drugs only when an episode of AF starts.

Paroxysmal atrial fibrillation

Episodes of AF that stop within 7 days, usually within 48 hours, without any treatment.
